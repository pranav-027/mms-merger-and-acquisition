# DOCUMENT 1: DEFENSIVE TACTICS IN ACQUISITIONS

## 1. INTRODUCTION TO DEFENSIVE TACTICS

### Definition

Defensive tactics are mechanisms adopted by **target companies** to **prevent** or **resist** mergers and acquisitions, particularly hostile takeovers.

### Purpose

- Safeguard **long-term interests of employees and shareholders**
- Protect company's **independent existence**
- Maintain **management control**
- Prevent loss of **strategic assets**

### Major Defensive Tactics (Quick List):

1. Divestures (Spin-off)
2. Crown Jewels
3. Poison Pill
4. Greenmail
5. White Knight
6. Golden Parachute

---

## 2. DIVESTURES / SPIN-OFFS (Detailed)

### Definition

The target company **divests or spins off** some of its businesses in the form of an **independent subsidiary**, thereby reducing the value and attractiveness of the existing business to the acquirer (bidder).

### How It Works

- Target identifies attractive business units
- Spins them off as independent entities
- Remaining company becomes less attractive
- Acquirer loses interest in pursuing takeover

### Real-World Examples:

**Example 1: ITT Corp**

- Spun off **Sheraton Hotels** and **gaming business Caesars World Casinos**
- Made it unattractive for **Hilton Hotels Corporation**'s takeover attempt

**Example 2: Reliance Industries**

- Spun off **Jio Platforms Ltd.**
- Global investors (Facebook/Meta, Silver Lake, KKR) invested in **Jio Platforms** (not directly in Reliance Industries)
- Control of the listed parent **did not change**

**Example 3: L&T**

- During Grasim Industries' takeover attempt
- L&T spun off its **cement division as UltraTech Cement**

### Advantages

- Reduces attractiveness to acquirer
- Unlocks shareholder value
- Allows focused management of subsidiaries
- Independent valuation of business units

### Disadvantages

- Loss of synergies
- Administrative complexity
- May reduce overall company size and influence

---

## 3. CROWN JEWELS (Detailed)

### Definition

When target firms **sell their most lucrative/valuable businesses** (the "crown jewels") to avoid being merged or taken over, it is said to **sell its crown jewels**.

### Strategy Logic

- The most valuable assets attract the acquirer
- By selling these assets, the target becomes less attractive
- Acquirer abandons takeover attempt

### Forms of Divestment:

- Third-party sale
- Spin-off
- Demerger
- Transfer to a friendly party (white knight)

### Classic Example: Crown Zellerbach Corporation vs Sir James Goldsmith

- Goldsmith attempted to take over Crown Zellerbach
- Crown sold off **Timberlands and Paper and Pulp division**
- Made the parent company less attractive
- Successfully thwarted the takeover

### Indian Example: Shaw Wallace

- Shaw Wallace faced hostile takeover threat from **Kishore Chhabria**
- Sold its **beer business (Haywards, Knockout, etc.)** to **SABMiller**
- Made remaining company less attractive

### Difference: Spin-off vs Crown Jewels

| Aspect           | Spin-off                   | Crown Jewels                       |
| ---------------- | -------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| **What is sold** | Any subsidiary             | Most valuable business             |
| **Purpose**      | Reduce attractiveness      | Eliminate target's main attraction |
| **Form**         | Usually independent entity | Outright sale possible             |

---

## 4. POISON PILL (Detailed)

### Definition

A strategy where a target company makes its shares **unfavorable** to the acquiring firm, thereby raising the cost of acquisition significantly and creating big disincentives.

### How It Works

- The acquired company may issue **substantial amount of convertible debentures** to existing shareholders
- When facing a takeover threat
- Makes acquisition prohibitively expensive

### Types of Poison Pills:

**(a) Flip-In Poison Pill**

- Existing shareholders (except acquirer) can buy more shares at discount
- Dilutes acquirer's stake

**(b) Flip-Over Poison Pill**

- Allows shareholders to buy acquirer's stock at discount post-merger
- Punishes acquirer post-acquisition

### Why Called "Poison Pill"?

- Like a pill that is **difficult to consume or accept**
- Makes the company **toxic** to the acquirer

### Effects:

- Significantly raises cost of acquisitions
- Creates big disincentives to deter attempts
- Forces acquirer to negotiate with management
- May completely deter hostile takeover

---

## 5. GREENMAIL (Detailed)

### Definition

Greenmail is a practice in corporate M&As where a **large block of stock** held by an **unfriendly company or raider** is **repurchased by the target company** at a **substantial premium** to destroy any takeover attempt.

### Etymology

- "Green" (money/cash) + "Mail" (blackmail) = Greenmail
- Refers to the cash premium paid to make raiders go away

### Process

1. Hostile raider acquires significant stake in target
2. Target company perceives takeover threat
3. Target offers to **buy back shares at premium**
4. Raider profits and exits
5. Takeover threat is eliminated

### Purpose

- End hostile takeover threat
- Maintain management control
- Protect existing shareholders' interests

### Criticism

- Benefits the raider/blackmailer
- Uses company funds (which belong to all shareholders)
- May encourage future raids

---

## 6. WHITE KNIGHT (Detailed)

### Definition

When the management of a target company offers to be acquired by a **friendly company** to escape from a **hostile takeover**, the friendly company is called a **White Knight**.

### Rationale

- Prevents loss of **managerial talent**
- Hostile bidder may **replace the management**
- White knight is more cooperative
- Better cultural fit

### How It Works

1. Hostile bidder approaches target
2. Target's management seeks alternative buyer
3. White knight offers to acquire on better terms
4. Original hostile bid is defeated
5. Friendly merger occurs

### Related Concept: White Squire

- Similar to white knight but acquires only **partial stake**
- Provides defensive support without full acquisition

### Advantages

- Saves managerial jobs
- Better terms for shareholders
- Cooperative integration
- Cultural compatibility

### Disadvantages

- Still results in loss of independence
- May not get best possible price

---

## 7. GOLDEN PARACHUTE (Detailed)

### Definition

When the company offers **hefty compensations** to its managers if they get **ousted due to takeover**, the company is said to offer **golden parachutes**.

### Triggers for Golden Parachute:

**Both events must occur:**

1. **Controlling stake** of the company changes hands
2. **Individual employees are terminated** from their jobs due to change in control of management

### Components of Golden Parachute:

- Severance pay (often multi-year salary)
- Stock options vesting
- Bonuses
- Insurance benefits
- Pension benefits

### Purpose

- **Protect senior executives** during takeover
- Retain talent during uncertainty
- Discourage hostile takeover (cost increases)

### Related Concepts:

**(a) Silver Parachute**

- Similar benefits for **middle management**

**(b) Tin Parachute**

- Benefits for **lower-level employees**

### Criticism

- May reward poor performance
- Conflict of interest (managers may approve takeover for personal gain)
- Reduces company value

# DOCUMENT 2: VALUATION OF TANGIBLES (DETAILED)

## 1. INTRODUCTION TO VALUATION

### Importance of Valuation

- Critical area in **corporate finance**
- Essential for **mergers and acquisitions**
- Helps identify sources of **economic value creation/destruction**
- Aids in strategic decision-making

### Value vs Price (Important Distinction)

| Value                          | Price                 |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------- |
| Different for different buyers | Single agreed amount  |
| Different for buyer and seller | Result of negotiation |
| Subjective assessment          | Objective outcome     |

### Example to Understand Value-Price Difference:

- A **foreign technologically-advanced company** buying a local company:
  - Values only its **brand** (for market reach)
  - Does NOT value plant and machinery
- The **seller**:
  - Gives more importance to **material resources**
  - Still uses them in manufacturing

### Buyer-Seller Perspectives:

- **Buyers:** Determine **maximum** value to pay
- **Sellers:** Determine **minimum** value to accept
- **Final price** = Between these two values

### Factors Affecting Negotiated Price:

1. Economies of scale
2. Economies of scope
3. Different perceptions about the industry
4. Different perceptions about the company

## 2. PURPOSES OF VALUATION

1. **Buying and selling** operations of companies

## 4. BALANCE SHEET METHODS (DETAILED)

### Assumptions:

- Company's value lies in its **balance sheet**
- Does NOT consider future value
- Does NOT consider:
  - Industry's current situation
  - Human resources/organizational problems
  - Contracts
  - These do not appear in accounting statements

### 4.1 BOOK VALUE METHOD

**Definition:** Net Worth as per books

**Formula:**
$$\text{Book Value} = \text{Total Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities}$$

**Example:** Net worth = ₹80 Million

---

### 4.2 ADJUSTED BOOK VALUE METHOD

**Definition:** Market value of assets and liabilities considered

**Adjustments:**

- Bad debts written off
- Stock revalued (discounting obsolete items)
- Fixed assets revalued at market value

**Solved Example:**

Given:

- Accounts Receivable includes ₹2 Million bad debt
- Stock value after adjustment = ₹52 Million
- Fixed Assets market value = ₹150 Million
- Cash = ₹8 Million; Other current assets = ₹5 Million
- Liabilities: 40 + 10 + 30

$$\text{Adjusted BV} = (150+52+8+5) - (40+10+30)$$
$$= 215 - 80 = ₹135 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 4.3 LIQUIDATION VALUE METHOD

**Definition:** Company's value if liquidated/sold off and debts paid, after deducting liquidation expenses.

**Key Point:** This represents the company's **MINIMUM value**.

**Formula:**
$$\text{Liquidation Value} = \text{Adjusted BV} - \text{Liquidation Expenses}$$

**Example:** If liquidation expenses = ₹60 Million
$$\text{Liquidation Value} = 135 - 60 = ₹75 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 4.4 SUBSTANTIAL VALUE METHOD

**Definition:** Investment required to form an identical company. Also known as **Asset Replacement Value**.

**Three Types:**

**(a) Gross Substantial Value**

- Only market value of assets
- = ₹215 Million

**(b) Net Substantial Value**

- Adjusted Book Value
- = ₹135 Million

**(c) Reduced Gross Substantial Value**

- Gross substantial value LESS cost-free debt
- = 215 - 40 = ₹175 Million

---

### 4.5 SUMMARY OF BALANCE SHEET METHODS

| Method                  | Value (₹ Million) |
| ----------------------- | ----------------- |
| Book Value              | 80                |
| Adjusted Book Value     | 135               |
| Liquidation Value       | 75                |
| Gross Substantial Value | 215               |
| Net Substantial Value   | 135               |
| Reduced Gross Value     | 175               |

**Range:** ₹75 Million to ₹215 Million
**Negotiating Power:** Drives final price

### Limitation

- BV typically has **little bearing** on market value
- Research (1992-2007): MPS/BPS = 1.5 to 6 times
- P/BV ratio increased from 1 (1990) to 3 (2007)

### Approach:

- Value determined through **size of earnings, sales**

---

### 5.1 VALUE OF EARNINGS (P/E RATIO METHOD)

**Formula:**
$$\text{Equity Value} = \text{PER} \times \text{Earnings}$$

**Example:**
If P/E = 8 times, Earnings = ₹26 Million
$$\text{Value} = 8 \times 26 = ₹108 \text{ Million}$$

> Note: P/E is taken from industry average

---

### 5.2 VALUE OF DIVIDENDS METHOD

**Formula:**
$$K_e = \frac{D}{P} \implies P = \frac{D}{K_e}$$

$$\text{Equity Value per Share} = \frac{DPS}{K_e}$$

**Example:**

- Dividends = ₹16 Million; Shares = 7 Million
- DPS = 16/7 = ₹2.29
- Cost of Equity (K_e) = 15%

$$\text{Equity Value per Share} = \frac{2.29}{0.15} = ₹15.27$$
$$\text{Total Value} = 15.27 \times 7 = ₹106.89 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 5.3 SALES MULTIPLES METHOD

**Formula:**
$$\text{Value} = \frac{P}{S} \times \text{Sales}$$

**Relationship:** P/S = (P/E) × (E/S)

**Example:**
P/S = 0.5; Sales = ₹300 Million
$$\text{Value} = 300 \times 0.5 = ₹150 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 5.4 OTHER MULTIPLES

- **P/EBIT** (Price to EBIT)
- **P/EBITDA** (Price to EBITDA)
- **P/Operating Cash Flow**
- **P/Book Value**

---

## 6. GOODWILL-BASED METHODS (DETAILED)

### General Formula:

$$V = A + GW$$

Where:

- V = Value of company
- A = Net Asset Value
- GW = Goodwill

---

### 6.1 CLASSIC VALUATION METHOD

**Formula:**
$$V = A + (n \times B) \quad \text{or} \quad V = A + (z \times F)$$

Where:

- A = Net asset value
- n = Coefficient (1.5 to 3)
- B = Net Income
- z = Percentage of sales revenue
- F = Turnover

**Example 1:** For Alfa Co., 3 times earnings = Goodwill

- Goodwill = 3 × 26 = ₹78 Million
  $$V = 135 + (3 \times 26) = ₹213 \text{ Million}$$

**Example 2:** Z = 50% of sales
$$V = 135 + (0.5 \times 300) = 135 + 150 = ₹285 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 6.2 SIMPLIFIED ABBREVIATED GOODWILL INCOME METHOD

**Formula:**
$$V = A + a_n(B - iA)$$

Where:

- a_n = Present value of annuity at rate t for n periods
- n = 5 to 8 years
- t = 15%
- i = 10% (risk-free rate)
- (B - iA) = Superprofit
- a_n × (B - iA) = Goodwill

**Example:**

- A = 135; B = 26; a_n = 3.352 (5 years @ 15%)
  $$V = 135 + 3.352 \times (26 - 0.10 \times 135)$$
  $$V = 135 + 3.352 \times (26 - 13.5)$$
  $$V = 135 + 3.352 \times 12.5$$
  $$V = 135 + 41.9 = ₹176.9 \text{ Million}$$

> a_n calculation: 1/1.15 + 1/1.15² + ... + 1/1.15⁵ = 3.352

---

### 6.3 UNION OF EUROPEAN ACCOUNTING EXPERTS METHOD

**Formula:**
$$V = A + a_n(B - iV)$$

Solving for V:
$$V = \frac{A + (a_n \times B)}{1 + i \times a_n}$$

**Example:**
$$V = \frac{135 + (3.352 \times 26)}{1 + (0.10 \times 3.352)}$$
$$V = \frac{135 + 87.15}{1.3352} = \frac{222.15}{1.3352} = ₹166.8 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 6.4 INDIRECT METHOD

**Formula:**
$$V = \frac{A + (B/i)}{2}$$

Where: A = Net Asset Value; B/i = Earnings Capitalisation Value

**Example:**
$$V = \frac{135 + (26/0.10)}{2} = \frac{135 + 260}{2} = ₹197.5 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 6.5 ANNUAL PROFIT PURCHASE METHOD

**Formula:**
$$V = A + m(B - iA)$$

Where: Goodwill = certain number of years of superprofits

**Example:** m = 5
$$V = 135 + 5 \times (26 - 0.10 \times 135)$$
$$V = 135 + 5 \times 12.5 = ₹197.5 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 6.6 RISK-BEARING AND RISK-FREE RATE METHOD

**Formula:**
$$V = A + \frac{(B - iV)}{t}$$

Solving for V:
$$V = \frac{A + (B/t)}{1 + (i/t)}$$

Where:

- i = Risk-free rate
- t = Risk-bearing rate

**Example:**
$$V = \frac{135 + (26/0.15)}{1 + (0.10/0.15)}$$
$$V = \frac{135 + 173.33}{1 + 0.667} = \frac{308.33}{1.667} = ₹185 \text{ Million}$$

---

## 7. CASH FLOW DISCOUNTING METHODS (DETAILED)

### 7.1 GENERAL METHOD FOR CASH FLOW DISCOUNTING

**Formula:**
$$V = \frac{CF_1}{(1+k_o)^1} + \frac{CF_2}{(1+k_o)^2} + ... + \frac{CF_n + VR_n}{(1+k_o)^n}$$

**Residual Value (Constant Growth):**
$$VR_n = \frac{CF_n(1+g)}{(k_o - g)}$$

---

### 7.2 DIVIDEND CAPITALISATION MODEL

#### (a) No Growth Model (g = 0)

$$V = \frac{D}{K_e}$$

**Example:** Dividend = ₹15; K_e = 20%
$$V = \frac{15}{0.20} = ₹75 \text{ per share}$$

#### (b) Constant Growth Model (Gordon Model)

$$V = \frac{D_1}{K_e - g}$$

**Example:** D = ₹15; K_e = 20%; g = 8%
$$V = \frac{15}{0.20 - 0.08} = ₹125 \text{ per share}$$

#### (c) Two-Stage Model

**Example:** D₁ = ₹15; K_e = 20%

- First 3 years g₁ = 8%
- From 4th year g₂ = 5%

Calculations:

- D₁ = ₹15
- D₂ = 15 × 1.08 = ₹16.2
- D₃ = 15 × (1.08)² = ₹17.50
- D₄ = D₃ × 1.05 = ₹18.375

Terminal Value at end of Year 3:
$$TV_3 = \frac{D_4}{K_e - g_2} = \frac{18.375}{0.15} = ₹122.5$$

Present Value calculations:

- PV of D₁ = 15/1.20 = 12.5
- PV of D₂ = 16.2/(1.20)² = 11.25
- PV of D₃ = 17.50/(1.20)³ = 10.125
- PV of TV₃ = 122.5/(1.20)³ = 70.89

$$V = 12.5 + 11.25 + 10.125 + 70.89 = ₹104.76 \text{ per share}$$

#### (d) Three-Stage Model

- First 3 years g₁ = 8%
- Next 2 years g₂ = 7%
- Remaining years g₃ = 3%

---

### 7.3 FREE CASH FLOW (FCF) METHOD

**Definition:** Cash flow available to all providers of capital (debt + equity)

**Formula:**
$$FCF = \text{EBIT}(1-T) + \text{Depreciation} - \text{Increase in FA} - \text{Increase in WC}$$

OR

$$FCF = \text{Net Income without Debt} + \text{Depreciation} - \Delta FA - \Delta WC$$

**Solved Example: FCF Calculation**

Income Statement:
| Particulars | 1999 | 2000 | 2001 |
|-------------|------|------|------|
| Sales | 1000 | 1100 | 1210 |
| - COGS | 650 | 715 | 786.5 |
| - Gen. Exp | 189 | 207.9 | 228.7 |
| - Depreciation | 20 | 20 | 20 |
| **EBIT** | **141** | **157.1** | **174.8** |
| - Interest | 10 | 10 | 10 |
| EBT | 131 | 147.1 | 164.8 |
| - Tax @35% | 45.85 | 51.49 | 57.68 |
| EAT | 85.15 | 95.62 | 107.10 |
| - Dividends | 34.06 | 38.25 | 42.85 |
| Retained Earnings | 51.09 | 57.37 | 64.28 |

FCF Calculation:
| Particulars | 1999 | 2000 | 2001 |
|-------------|------|------|------|
| EBIT | 141 | 157.1 | 174.8 |
| - Tax on EBIT (35%) | 49.35 | 55.00 | 61.20 |
| **EBIT(1-T)** | **91.65** | **102.10** | **113.60** |
| + Depreciation | 20 | 20 | 20 |
| - Increase in FA | 61 | 67.10 | 73.80 |
| - Increase in WCR | 11 | 12.10 | 13.30 |
| **Free Cash Flow** | **39.65** | **42.90** | **46.50** |

**Valuation:**
$$\text{Value of Firm} = \frac{FCF}{k_o}$$

If WACC = 20%, FCF = 46.51:
$$\text{Value} = \frac{46.51}{0.20} = \$232.55 \text{ Million}$$

---

### 7.4 EQUITY CASH FLOW (ECF) METHOD

**Formula:**
$$ECF = FCF - \text{Interest}(1-T) - \text{Principal Repayments} + \text{New Debt}$$

**Valuation:**
$$\text{Value of Equity} = \frac{ECF}{K_e}$$

**Cost of Equity:**
$$K_e = \frac{D_1}{P} + g$$
OR
$$K_e = R_f + \beta(R_m - R_f) \quad \text{(CAPM)}$$

---

### 7.5 CAPITAL CASH FLOW (CCF) METHOD

**Formula:**
$$CCF = ECF + DCF = ECF + I - \Delta \text{Debt}$$

> Note: CCF and FCF are different; both are discounted at WACC

---

### 7.6 VALUE OF LEVERED FIRM

**Formula:**
$$V_L = V_U + TD$$

Where:

- V_L = Value of levered firm
- V_U = Value of unlevered firm (FCF/k_u)
- TD = Present Value of Interest Tax Shield

**Key Point:** Levered firm has **higher value** than unlevered firm due to tax shield benefits.

# DOCUMENT 3: VALUATION OF INTANGIBLES (DETAILED)

## 1. INTRODUCTION TO INTANGIBLE ASSETS

### Definition (Investopedia)

An intangible asset is an asset that is **not physical in nature**. Includes:

- Patents
- Trademarks
- Copyrights
- Business methodologies
- Goodwill
- Brand recognition

### Key Characteristics

## 16. ECONOMIC CONSIDERATIONS FOR BRANDS

Consumers perceive value in brands when:

### (a) Cost-Driven Brands

- Cost less than competing brands with identical features
- Selling Price (Firm) < Selling Price (Competitor)
- Cost (Firm) < Cost (Competitor)
- Profit per unit (Firm) = Profit per unit (Competitor)

**Formula:** SC - SF = CC - CF

### (b) Value-Added Brands

- Unique benefits offsetting premium price
- Selling Price (Firm) > Selling Price (Competitor)
- Cost (Firm) > Cost (Competitor)
- Profit also greater than competitor

> Note: When premium is too high, careful brand valuation is essential.

## 21. TECHNIQUES OF BRAND VALUATION (DETAILED)

### 21.1 HISTORIC COST MODEL

**Method:** Aggregated investment cost (capital expenditure)

**Components:**

- Marketing and distribution expenditure
- Advertising and promotional expenditure
- R&D expenditure
- Adjusted for past inflation

**Demerit:** Does NOT give current value of brand

---

### 21.2 PREMIUM PRICE MODEL

**Formula:**
$$\text{Brand Value} = (P_{branded} - P_{non-branded}) \times \text{Volume of Sales}$$

**Example:**

- Branded price = ₹1000
- Non-branded price = ₹500
- Difference × Quantity sold = Brand Value

**Limitation:** Higher price may not always mean better brand

---

### 21.3 MARKET VALUE MODEL

**Assumption:** Existence of a market for brands where they can be bought, sold, and compared.

**Limitation:** Brands are not developed for trading purposes.

---

### 21.4 SENSITIVITY MODEL

**Formula:**
$$\text{Brand Value} = (\text{Estimated Brand Revenue} \times A \times R \times E) - \text{Historical Cost}$$

Where:

- Brand Revenue = Brand units sold × Brand price
- **A** = Awareness
- **R** = Recognition
- **E** = Esteem

---

### 21.5 REPLACEMENT COST MODEL

**Method:** Estimated replacement cost of identical substitute brands

**Limitations:**

- Subjective
- Ignores distinctive features of brands

---

### 21.6 DISCOUNTED CASH FLOW MODEL

**Method:** Future cash flow of brand discounted at appropriate rate

**Considerations:**

- Prediction period follows product life cycle
- Market changes may end product life unexpectedly
- Discount rate should consider inflation and risk factors
- Brand-wise cash flow easier nowadays (segment reporting)

---

### 21.7 INTERBRAND MODEL (MOST IMPORTANT)

### Background

**Interbrand** is the **pioneer** of brand valuation. Uses an economic method based on the premise that **well-managed brands influence consumer behavior** and affect profits.

### Four Elements of Interbrand Valuation:

**(1) Financial Analysis**

- Identify earnings for each segment

**(2) Market Analysis**

- Proportion of earnings from intangibles attributable to brand
- Measured by **Role of Branding Index**

**(3) Brand Analysis**

- Assess competitive strengths and weaknesses
- Measured by **Brand Strength Score**

**(4) Legal Analysis**

- Establish brand as a true piece of "property"

---

### Brand Equity Formula

**Brand Equity** exists if **excess cash flow streams** result from putting a particular brand name on a product.

### Brand Attributes (6):

1. **Awareness** - Recognition, recall, dominance, knowledge
2. **Image** - Perception of brand's characteristics
3. **Perceived Quality** - Comparing with competitors
4. **Perceived Value** - Performance expectations vs price
5. **Personality** - Brand's self-expressive capabilities
6. **Organizational Associations** - Perceptions from parent organization

### Brand Strengths (7) - LSMITSP Formula:

$$\text{Brand Value} = (L + S + M + I + T + S + P)$$

Where:

- **L** = Leadership
- **S** = Stability (well-established brands)
- **M** = Market (future trends of market share)
- **I** = Internationality (attract international customers)
- **T** = Trend (long-term relevance)
- **S** = Support (spends to maintain quality)
- **P** = Protection (registration, trademark)

---

### Interbrand Method (Earnings Multiple Model)

**Formula:**
$$\boxed{\text{Brand Value} = \text{Brand Earning} \times \text{Brand Multiple}}$$

**Brand Strength Factors and Maximum Scores:**

| Factor           | Maximum Score |
| ---------------- | ------------- |
| Leadership       | 25            |
| Stability        | 15            |
| Market           | 10            |
| Internationality | 25            |
| Trend            | 10            |
| Support          | 10            |
| Protection       | 5             |
| **Total Score**  | **100**       |

### Dimensions Explained with Examples:

| Dimension        | Meaning                       | Example    |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------- | ---------- |
| Leadership       | Market leader                 | Apple      |
| Stability        | Consistent performance        | Coca-Cola  |
| Market           | Strong market share           | Nike       |
| Internationality | Global recognition            | McDonald's |
| Trend            | Modern/Innovative             | Tesla      |
| Support          | Marketing/operational backing | Samsung    |
| Protection       | Legal & IP safeguards         | Disney     |

### Key Points:

- **Interbrand considers maximum multiple of 20**
- **Higher brand score → Higher brand multiple** (max 20)
- Relationship between brand strength and brand value multiple is an **S-shaped curve**

---

## 22. SOLVED NUMERICAL: INTERBRAND METHOD

**Problem:**
Calculate Brand Value using Interbrand method.

Given:

- Brand Earnings = ₹50 million
- Brand Strength Scores:
  - Leadership: 20
  - Stability: 12
  - Market: 8
  - Internationality: 18
  - Trend: 8
  - Support: 7
  - Protection: 4

**Solution:**

**Step 1: Calculate Total Brand Strength Score**
$$\text{Total Score} = 20 + 12 + 8 + 18 + 8 + 7 + 4 = 77/100$$

**Step 2: Determine Brand Multiple**
Based on S-curve, score of 77 corresponds to multiple of approximately **15** (max being 20)

**Step 3: Calculate Brand Value**
$$\text{Brand Value} = 50 \times 15 = ₹750 \text{ Million}$$

## SAMPLE NUMERICAL PROBLEMS

### Problem 1: Adjusted Book Value

**Question:** Given Total Assets ₹500 mn, Liabilities ₹200 mn. Adjustments: Bad debts ₹10 mn, Stock revalued from ₹80 mn to ₹70 mn, Fixed Assets revalued upward by ₹50 mn. Calculate Adjusted Book Value.

**Solution:**

- Original BV = 500 - 200 = ₹300 mn
- Adjustments: -10 (bad debts) -10 (stock) +50 (FA) = +30 mn
- **Adjusted BV = 300 + 30 = ₹330 mn**

### Problem 2: P/E Method

**Question:** Net Income = ₹40 mn, Industry P/E = 12. Find Equity Value.

**Solution:**
**Equity Value = 12 × 40 = ₹480 mn**

### Problem 3: Gordon Growth Model

**Question:** D₁ = ₹5, K_e = 14%, g = 8%. Find share value.

**Solution:**
$$V = \frac{5}{0.14 - 0.08} = \frac{5}{0.06} = ₹83.33 \text{ per share}$$

### Problem 4: FCF and Firm Value

**Question:** EBIT = ₹100 mn, Tax = 30%, Depreciation = ₹15 mn, Increase in FA = ₹40 mn, Increase in WC = ₹10 mn, WACC = 12%.

**Solution:**

- EBIT(1-T) = 100 × 0.70 = 70 mn
- FCF = 70 + 15 - 40 - 10 = ₹35 mn
- **Firm Value = 35 / 0.12 = ₹291.67 mn**

### Problem 5: Interbrand Method

**Question:** Brand Earnings = ₹100 mn. Brand Strength: L=22, S=13, M=8, I=20, T=9, S=8, P=4. Brand multiple from S-curve = 17.

**Solution:**

- Total Score = 22+13+8+20+9+8+4 = **84/100**
- **Brand Value = 100 × 17 = ₹1,700 mn**

### Problem 6: Premium Price Model

**Question:** Branded price ₹150, Non-branded ₹100, Volume 50,000 units. Calculate brand value.

**Solution:**
**Brand Value = (150-100) × 50,000 = ₹25 lakhs**
