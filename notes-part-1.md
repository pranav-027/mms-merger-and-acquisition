# 📖 DOCUMENT 1: MERGERS, TYPES, ADVANTAGES, DISADVANTAGES & CONCERNS

## 🔹 1. INTRODUCTION TO MERGERS

### Definition

A **merger** is a combination of two companies into one. Mergers can occur through:

- **Absorption**
- **Consolidation**

### 1.1 Absorption

- One corporation is completely **absorbed** by another
- The less important company **loses its identity**
- The surviving (more important) company **retains its identity**
- It assumes all rights, privileges, and liabilities of the merged corporation

**Examples:**

- Hindustan Lever Ltd. + Tata Oil Mills Company
- ICICI Bank + Bank of Madura

### 1.2 Consolidation

- Two or more companies combine to form a **completely new company**
- Both original companies lose their identity

**Examples:**

- HCL Limited (formed from Hindustan Computers Ltd, Hindustan Instruments Ltd, Indian Software Co. Ltd, Indian Reprographics Ltd)
- Vodafone Idea

> 📝 **Note:** In India, mergers are legally called **"Amalgamations"**

---

## 🔹 2. ACQUISITION

### Definition

An acquisition is when one company **purchases most/all** of another company's shares to gain control (more than 50% ownership).

### Key Features:

- Acquiring company can make decisions **without approval** of target's shareholders
- Often involves purchasing stock of the target

**Examples:**
| Acquirer | Target | Details |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Vodafone | Hutch-Essar | 67% stake for $11.1 billion |
| LIC | IDBI Bank | 51% for ₹14,500 crores |
| Flipkart | Myntra | 100% for ~₹2,000 crores |
| Walmart | Flipkart | 77% for $16 billion |
| Ola | TaxiForSure | ₹1,200 crores |

---

## 🔹 3. TYPES OF MERGERS ⭐ (Important for Theory)

### (A) Vertical Merger

**Definition:** Merger of companies at **different stages** of supply chain.

**Two Types:**

1. **Backward Integration** – Towards source of supply
   - _Example:_ Reliance (textiles → polyester → petrochemicals → oil exploration)
2. **Forward Integration** – Towards market outlets
   - _Example:_ Apple opening retail stores

**Benefits:**

- Strong market position
- Control over product specifications
- Increased synergy & supply chain control
- Reduced costs, higher productivity

**Examples:**

- eBay acquiring PayPal (2002)
- Goenka Group's CEAT Tyres acquiring Harrisons Malayalam (rubber supply)

---

### (B) Horizontal Merger

**Definition:** Merger of two **competing firms** at the **same stage** of industrial process.

**Purposes:**

- Economies of scale
- Broaden product line
- Reduce working capital
- Eliminate competition
- Reduce advertising costs
- Better market control

**Examples:**

- Exxon-Mobil merger (1998/99)
- Daimler-Benz + Chrysler
- Lipton India + Brooke Bond India

---

### (C) Circular Combination

**Definition:** Companies producing **distinct products** merge to share **common distribution/research facilities**.

**Benefits:**

- Resource sharing economies
- Diversification
- Market enlargement

**Example:** HLL + Brooke Bond Lipton India Ltd (1996) – overlap in distribution & technology base

---

### (D) Conglomerate Combination

**Definition:** Amalgamation of companies in **unrelated industries**.

**Two Types:**

1. **Pure Conglomerate** – Firms with nothing in common
2. **Mixed Conglomerate** – Product/market extensions

**Purposes:**

- Utilization of financial resources
- Lower average cost of capital
- Enhance overall stability

**Example:** Walt Disney + American Broadcasting Company

---

### (E) Other Types

- **Market-Extension Merger:** Same products in different markets (e.g., ICICI + Bank of Madura)
- **Product-Extension Merger:** Different but related products in common market (e.g., Pepsi + Tropicana)

## 🔹 5. REASONS COMPANIES MERGE ⭐

### 🟢 Plausible (Value-Creating) Reasons:

1. **Synergy Effect** – V(AB) > VA + VB
2. **Strategic Benefit** – Pre-emptive moves, leap-frogging
3. **Economies of Scale** – Larger volume operations
4. **Economies of Scope** – Wider activity scope
5. **Vertical Integration Economies** – Better coordination (e.g., ONGC + HPCL)
6. **Complementary Resources** – e.g., ABB (Asea Brown Bovery)
7. **Tax Shields** – Loss firm's losses set off against profit firm
8. **Utilization of Surplus Funds** – Mature industry cash use
9. **Managerial Effectiveness** – Replace poor management

### 🔴 Dubious (Doubtful) Reasons:

1. **Diversification** – Investors can do "homemade" diversification
2. **Lower Financing Costs** – Marginal benefit only
3. **Increased Supply-Chain Pricing Power**
4. **Eliminate Competition** – Often requires high premiums

## 🔹 6. MOTIVATIONS FOR M&A (Stakeholder Analysis)

### (1) Shareholders' Standpoint

- Monopoly profits
- Economies of scale
- Diversification
- Better investment opportunities

### (2) Managers' Standpoint

- Better perks & status
- Career growth
- ⚠️ Fear of displacement

### (3) Promoters' Gains

- Convert private to public company
- Without losing control
- Increase company size

### (4) General Public

- **Consumers:** Lower prices, better quality
- **Workers:** Mixed impact – job opportunities vs. layoffs
- **General:** Concerns about monopoly power

---

## 🔹 7. CONCERNS OF MERGERS ⭐ (Theory Question)

### Horizontal Mergers – 3 Competitive Problems:

1. **Elimination of competition** between merging firms
2. **Market power creation** – ability to raise prices
3. **Increased concentration** – tacit coordination (Cartel-like behavior)

### Vertical Mergers Concerns:

1. **Foreclosure** – limit competitor access to suppliers/customers
2. **Entry barriers** – impede new businesses

### Conglomerate Mergers Concerns:

1. May lessen future competition
2. Convert large firm into dominant one
3. Reduce number of smaller firms
4. Increase political power of merged firm

> 📌 **Competition Commission of India (CCI):** Approves M&A under "Green Channel" route for faster approval if no overlaps exist.

## 🔹 4. IMPACT OF M&A ON CORPORATE PERFORMANCE (Bhoi, RBI 2000)

### Two Approaches to Measure Impact:

1. **Event Studies** – Monitor share prices post-M&A
2. **Profitability Studies** – Compare profitability before/after

### Key Findings:

- Most firms **don't show significant long-term profitability** improvement
- **Conglomerate M&As** > Horizontal/Vertical (Reid, Mueller)
- **Cross-border M&As** may outperform domestic but have high failure rate
- **LBOs/MBOs** show significant efficiency improvements
- Productivity (not just profitability) shows improvement post-M&A

## 🔹 5. THE M&A PROCESS – 5 STAGES ⭐ (Important for Theory)

### **Stage 1: Screening Potential Deals**

- Look at everything (Cypress Group: 500 prospects → 25 examined → 3 deals)
- Maintain strategic focus
- Think strategically, act opportunistically

### **Stage 2: Reaching an Initial Agreement**

**Three Key Points:**

1. Don't get bogged down over price (incomplete information)
2. **Identify Must-Haves:**
   - Management experience
   - Incentive structure
   - CEO roles (e.g., American Home Products + Monsanto failure)
   - Potential liabilities (environmental, healthcare)
3. **Get Friendly** – Build relationship capital

### **Stage 3: Conducting Due Diligence**

- Most time-consuming, least creative
- Boredom is dangerous!
  **Key Activities:**

1. **Turn over all the rocks** – A deal that dies at due diligence dies for the right reasons
2. **Size up the other side** – Assess management abilities, teamwork, motivation
3. **Feed due diligence into business planning** – Build valuation model

### **Stage 4: Getting to Final Terms**

**Key Strategies:**

1. Use multiple negotiation channels (managers, lawyers, bankers) – parallel processing
2. **Cultivate alternatives** – BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)
3. **Anticipate competition** – Other bidders (e.g., IBM's bid for Lotus)

### **Stage 5: Closing**

- Final execution

# 📖 DOCUMENT 3: LEGAL PROCEDURE FOR MERGERS ⭐

## 🔹 STEP-BY-STEP LEGAL PROCESS

### **Step 1: Examination of Object Clauses**

- Check MOA of both companies for power to amalgamate
- Object clause must permit merging company to carry on merged company's business
- If absent → approval needed from shareholders, BOD, Company Law Board

### **Step 2: Approval of Draft Merger Proposal**

- Both BODs must approve
- Pass resolution authorizing directors/executives

### **Step 3: Intimation to Stock Exchanges**

- Inform stock exchanges where companies are listed
- Send copies of all notices, resolutions, orders

### **Step 4: Application to High Courts**

- Apply to HC of state where registered office is located
- For convening shareholders & creditors meetings

### **Step 5: Dispatch of Notices**

- Send notice + explanatory statement to shareholders & creditors
- Minimum **21 days** advance intimation
- Publish in **2 newspapers**

### **Step 6: Holding of Meetings**

- Shareholders' meeting needed
- **75% approval** required (in person or by proxy)
- Same applies to creditors

### **Step 7: Petition to High Court for Confirmation**

- File petition for confirmation
- Publish notice in 2 newspapers

### **Step 8: Filing the Order with Registrar**

- File certified true copies of HC order with Registrar of Companies (within specified time)

### **Step 9: Transfer of Assets and Liabilities**

- After final orders → transfer all assets & liabilities

### **Step 10: Issue of Shares and Debentures**

- Merging company issues shares/debentures to merged company shareholders
- New shares listed on stock exchange

- **Purpose:** Ensure acquirer pays shareholders the agreed amount

# 📖 DOCUMENT 4: SWAP RATIOS ⭐⭐ (Important for Numericals)

## 🔹 KEY CONCEPTS

### Swap Ratio Definition:

The ratio at which an acquiring company offers its own shares in exchange for the target company's shares.

### Three Bases for Calculating Swap Ratios:

1. **Market Price per Share (MPS)**
2. **Earnings per Share (EPS)**
3. **Book Value per Share (BPS)**

---

## 🔹 KEY FORMULAS ⭐

### Swap Ratio Formulas:

$$\text{Swap Ratio (MPS)} = \frac{MPS_{Selling}}{MPS_{Buying}}$$

$$\text{Swap Ratio (EPS)} = \frac{EPS_{Selling}}{EPS_{Buying}}$$

$$\text{Swap Ratio (BPS)} = \frac{BPS_{Selling}}{BPS_{Buying}}$$

### Post-Merger EPS:

$$EPS_{Post-Merger} = \frac{\text{Total Earnings (A + B + Synergy)}}{\text{Total Shares (Existing + New Issued)}}$$

### Number of New Shares Issued:

$$\text{New Shares} = \text{Swap Ratio} \times \text{No. of Shares of Target Company}$$

---

## 🔹 SOLVED EXAMPLE ⭐

**Given:** A Ltd. acquiring B Ltd.

| Particulars               | A Ltd.  | B Ltd.  |
| ------------------------- | ------- | ------- |
| Equity Capital (₹10 each) | ₹450 mn | ₹180 mn |
| Earnings                  | ₹90 mn  | ₹18 mn  |
| MPS                       | ₹60     | ₹37     |
| No. of Shares             | 45 mn   | 18 mn   |

Synergy: 10% increase in earnings post-merger

### **Solution:**

**(i) Swap Ratio based on MPS:**
$$= \frac{37}{60} = 0.62$$

> For every 1 share in B, shareholders get 0.62 shares in A

**(ii) Swap Ratio based on EPS:**

- EPS of A = 90/45 = ₹2/share
- EPS of B = 18/18 = ₹1/share
  $$\text{Swap Ratio} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$$

**(iii) EPS of A before merger** = ₹2/share

**(iv) Post-Merger EPS (Based on MPS):**

- New shares issued = 18 × 0.62 = 11.16 mn
- Total earnings = 90 + 18 + 10% synergy = ₹118.8 mn
  $$EPS = \frac{108 + 10.8}{45 + 11.16} = ₹2.12/share$$

**(v) Post-Merger EPS (Based on EPS):**

- New shares issued = 18 × 0.5 = 9 mn
  $$EPS = \frac{108 + 10.8}{45 + 9} = ₹2.20/share$$

**(vi) Recommendation:** ✅ **Merger should be undertaken** as post-merger EPS (₹2.20 or ₹2.12) > pre-merger EPS (₹2.00)

---

## 🔹 KEY POINTS FOR SWAP RATIO

- **Manufacturing companies** (high tangible fixed assets) → BPS preferred
- **Software/Service companies** → MPS or future earnings preferred
- Companies often use **weighted average** of all 3 ratios
- Choice of base depends on **sector**

# 📖 DOCUMENT 5: COSTS & BENEFITS OF MERGER ⭐⭐

## 🔹 KEY CONCEPT

**For Buying Company (A):** Capital INVESTMENT decision
**For Target Company (B):** Capital DISINVESTMENT decision

Both must calculate **NPV** of the decision.

---

## 🔹 ACCOUNTING TREATMENT EXAMPLES

### Case 1: Goodwill

- Net Worth of B = ₹10,00,000
- Buying price = ₹13,00,000
- **Excess ₹3,00,000 = Goodwill**

### Case 2: Capital Reserve

- Net Worth of B = ₹10,00,000
- Buying price = ₹8,00,000
- **Discount ₹2,00,000 = Capital Reserve**

### Case 3: Purchase Consideration Calculation

- Assets = ₹13,00,000
- Outside Liabilities = ₹8,00,000
- **Purchase Consideration = ₹5,00,000**

---

## 🔹 KEY FORMULAS ⭐⭐⭐

### 1. Benefit of Merger (Synergy):

$$\boxed{\text{Benefit} = PV_{AB} - (PV_A + PV_B)}$$

### 2. Cost of Merger (Cash Compensation):

$$\boxed{\text{Cost} = \text{Cash} - PV_B}$$

### 3. Cost of Merger (Stock Compensation):

$$\boxed{\text{Cost} = a \cdot PV_{AB} - PV_B}$$

Where: **a = fraction of combined entity received by B's shareholders**
$$a = \frac{\text{New Shares Issued to B}}{\text{Total Shares After Merger}}$$

### 4. NPV to Acquirer (A):

$$\boxed{NPV_A = \text{Benefit} - \text{Cost}}$$

### 5. NPV to Target (B):

$$\boxed{NPV_B = \text{Cost to A} = \text{Cash} - PV_B}$$

### 6. Combined Value:

$$PV_{AB} = PV_A + PV_B + \text{Benefit}$$

---

## 🔹 SOLVED EXAMPLE 1: CASH COMPENSATION ⭐

**Given:**
| Particulars | A | B |
|-------------|-----|-----|
| MPS | ₹50 | ₹20 |
| No. of Shares | 5,00,000 | 2,50,000 |
| Market Value | ₹25 mn | ₹5 mn |

Synergy gain = ₹5 mn | Cash compensation = ₹8 mn

### Solution:

**Cost of Merger:**
$$\text{Cost} = 8 - 5 = ₹3 \text{ mn}$$

**NPV to A:**
$$NPV_A = (35 - 30) - (8 - 5) = 5 - 3 = ₹2 \text{ mn}$$

**NPV to B:**
$$NPV_B = 8 - 5 = ₹3 \text{ mn}$$

✅ **Merger acceptable to both A and B**

---

## 🔹 SOLVED EXAMPLE 2: STOCK COMPENSATION ⭐

**Same data**, but A offers 1,25,000 shares to B's shareholders.

### Solution:

**Calculate 'a':**
$$a = \frac{1,25,000}{5,00,000 + 1,25,000} = 0.2$$

**Calculate PV_AB:**
$$PV_{AB} = 25 + 5 + 5 = ₹35 \text{ mn}$$

**Cost of Merger:**
$$\text{Cost} = (0.2 \times 35) - 5 = 7 - 5 = ₹2 \text{ mn}$$

**NPV to A:**
$$NPV_A = 5 - 2 = ₹3 \text{ mn}$$

**NPV to B:**
$$NPV_B = ₹2 \text{ mn}$$

✅ **Merger acceptable**

---

## 🔹 SOLVED EXAMPLE 3: WHEN MERGER IS NOT ACCEPTABLE

**Same data**, but A offers 2,50,000 shares.

### Solution:

$$a = \frac{2,50,000}{5,00,000 + 2,50,000} = 0.33$$

**Cost of Merger:**
$$\text{Cost} = (0.33 \times 35) - 5 = ₹6.55 \text{ mn}$$

**NPV to A:**
$$NPV_A = 5 - 6.55 = -₹1.55 \text{ mn}$$ ❌

**Conclusion:** ❌ **Merger NOT acceptable to A** as cost (₹6.55 mn) exceeds synergy gains (₹5 mn).

---

## 🔹 SOLVED EXAMPLE 4: NEGATIVE COST (BENEFIT TO BUYER)

**Given:**
| Particulars | A | B |
|-------------|-----|-----|
| MPS | ₹60 | ₹15 |
| No. of Shares | 1,00,000 | 50,000 |
| Market Value | ₹60,00,000 | ₹7,50,000 |

Cash payment = ₹10,00,000 | Synergy = Assumed

### Solution:

**Cost of Merger:**
$$\text{Cost} = 10,00,000 - 7,50,000 = ₹2,50,000$$

**NPV to A:**
$$NPV_A = \text{Benefit} - 2,50,000 = -₹2,50,000$$
(Assuming benefit = 0, since no synergy mentioned)

**NPV to B:**
$$NPV_B = ₹2,50,000$$
